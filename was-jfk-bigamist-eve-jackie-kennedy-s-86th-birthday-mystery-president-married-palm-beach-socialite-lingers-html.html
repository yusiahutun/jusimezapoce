Was President John F. Kennedy a bigamist?

 Posted on February 26, 2024  |  12 minutes  |  2373 words  |  Jenniffer Sheldon

New York Times bestselling author of The Other Mrs. Kennedy: An Intimate and Revealing Look at the Hidden Life of Ethel Skakel Kennedy and the upcoming RFK Jr.: Robert F. Kennedy, Jr. and the Dark Side of the Dream investigates President John F. Kennedy's relationship with Durie Malcolm.

Beloved First Lady Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis would turn 86 on July 28, but 21 years after her death in May, 1994 a mystery still lingers over whether she was the first, or actually the second wife of President John Kennedy - or even married to him at all.

For years stories circulated that the handsome future president who married Jacqueline Bouvier on September 12, 1953, had first secretly married beautiful blonde Palm Beach socialite Durie Malcolm on a lark in early 1947.

This month, as the late Jack and Jackie's surviving daughter, U.S. ambassador to Japan Caroline Kennedy Schlossberg, and other members of America's so-called royal family mourn Jackie's death from cancer, there is evidence that suggests the records of JFK's alleged secret marriage were destroyed at the behest of his father, the powerful Kennedy patriarch Joseph P. Kennedy. 

And later the Kennedy brothers Jack and Robert used their influence to stifle press investigations into the reputed betrothal to avoid political scandal.

According to one of JFK's sisters, the couple went off an got married after a drunken party in Palm Beach in 1947.

Not unexpectedly, Jack Kennedy, then being primed by his father for the presidency, denied marrying Durie, but did so when reporters began chasing the marriage rumors. However, he did acknowledge that he and Durie had briefly dated.

So, was the story of a JFK secret,  although brief, marriage a 'falsehoodâ€¦a curious footnote to the Kennedy legend,' as Clifford wrote in a memoir, or was there fire where there was smoke?

In the realm of the many Kennedy clan scandals â€“ from Ted Kennedy's Chappaquiddick affair in which a young woman died, to Robert Kennedy's son Michael's illicit romance with a teenage babysitter, to Kennedy cousin Michael Skakel's role in a gruesome murder â€“ a secret marriage by a young skirt-chasing Jack Kennedy to a pretty girl doesn't seem off the charts.

Pulitzer Prize-winning investigative reporter Seymour M. Hersh, who deeply probed the Kennedy-Malcolm story and gave it credence in his book 'The Dark Side of Camelot,' told Daily Mail Online that despite the pro forma denials he stands by what his sources told him, that there was such a marriage. 

'I wrote what I wrote,' he said.

Hersh tracked down what appear to be credible Kennedy insiders, among them a man named Charles Spalding, who claimed that he was instructed by JFK, with whom he had been close buddies since their World War II service, to make the marriage records disappear.

Hersh quoted Spalding, a retired Wall Street stockbroker, as telling him that the Kennedy patriarch had a virtual 'hemorrhage' after learning of the secret marriage of his son, then a Massachusetts congressman, whom he was grooming for the presidency. 

Joe Kennedy 'demanded that it [records of the marriage] be taken care of. They [the Kennedy family] were afraid the whole thing was going to come out. I went out there and removed the [marriage] papers. It was Jack who asked me if I'd go get the papers.'

According to Hersh, Spalding had the assistance of an unidentified Palm Beach lawyer in securing the marriage documents from what was presumed to be the Palm Beach County courthouse. Marriage records in those days were recorded by hand, not filed in alphabetical order, making it all but impossible to determine if any records were missing.

Beyond a record of marriage, there was no record ever found of a Jack Kennedy-Durie Malcolm divorce.

Hersh raises the possibility, therefore, that if there was a marriage and no divorce that John Fitzgerald Kennedy and Durie Malcolm -- who had had two previous marriages ending in divorce, and would have two more -- 'were bigamists.'

He quoted an unnamed Palm Beach high society matron friend of Kennedy as telling him not to write about the marriage of Kennedy and Malcolm.

She said, 'Jack was never married legally to Jackie, and all that's left of Jack are his two children [John Kennedy Jr. subsequently tragically died in a plane crash, leaving only Caroline] and his grandchildren.'

The woman warned Hersh, he claimed, that if he wrote about the Kennedy-Malcolm marriage it would make John Jr. and Caroline 'not legal. That's a very disruptive thing to do. Those children had a hard enough time as it is â€“ they lost their father.'

There were a number of others who were certain there had been a secret Kennedy marriage, among them even one of Jack Kennedy's sisters, Eunice Kennedy Shriver, who once reportedly told Charles Spalding's wife, Betty, that 'there was a drunken party and they [JFK and Durie] went off to a justice of the peace to get married,' and that it was all the fault of the bride. 

According to Hersh, Eunice claimed to Betty Spalding that Durie Malcolm 'wouldn't sleep' with JFK unless he made her an honest woman by marrying her.

The Spalding woman later claimed having heard that Joe Kennedy, whom she described as the Old Man, 'had a s*** fit' and got his son's marriage 'nullified.' 

By the middle of 1947, Durie Malcolm married again, her third officially known betrothal, to a wealthy Palm Beach socialite by the name of Thomas Shevlin. 

Durie had one child, a daughter named after her. Six months after JFK was assassinated in November 1963, she married the son of a retired major in the British Coldstream Guards, and moved to Great Britain, and declined to ever discuss the Kennedy affair.

Malcolm denied that she and Kennedy were ever wed. A dozen years before her death in Palm Beach in 2008, a widow at the age of 91, she rudely claimed, 'I wouldn't have married Jack Kennedy for all the tea in Chinaâ€¦If you want to know the truth, I didn't care for those Irish micks, and old Joe was a terrible man.' 

She also once signed a sworn affidavit, attesting that there had been no marriage, signed reportedly at the behest of, or pressure from, JFK's crises manager and fixer, the prominent Washington lawyer Clark Clifford.

But a Shevlin family member told Hersh that Durie, despite her JFK marriage denials, once confirmed to her that she and Jack Kennedy had, in fact, been married, and that Durie was led to believe that Joe Kennedy had 'gone ballistic and got everything [the records of the marriage] eradicated â€“ all the records wiped out.' 

It was known within the family, the relative claimed, that JFK 'really loved' Durie Malcolm 'and wanted to be with her.'

Many men did pursue her as evidenced by her four acknowledged marriages. The Palm Beach lovely was as different as night and day from philandering JFK's official first lady, according to Charles Spalding, who died in 1999. 

Spalding had known Durie from childhood when she was growing up in an affluent Chicago suburb. She was very unlike Jackie, who was the brunt of jokes and teasing by JFK's rambunctious sisters, and especially from her rough and tumble sister-in-law Ethel Skakel Kennedy, because she didn't join in the clan's fabled touch football games and was viewed as too girly.

Spalding remembered Durie as a guy's kind of girl who loved sports, more like a Kennedy woman. 'She played ice hockey in the winter and basketball with guys,' he recalled. Moreover, 'She got into men in a big way. She had plenty of men hanging around.'

As Hersh noted, Jack Kennedy was 'infatuated.'

A society gossip column in the New York World-Telegram, dated January 20, 1947, gave the news that Kennedy was in line to get the Palm Beach 'annual Oscar for achievement in the field of romance' by giving beautiful and funny Durie Malcolm 'the season's outstanding rush'.

Only the fact that duty called him to Washington as a Congressman from Massachusetts kept Jack from staying around to receive his Oscar in person, so it may be awarded to Durie. 

The two were inseparable at all social functions and sports events. They even drove down to Miami to hold hands at football games and wager on the horses.

First time out: Durie Malcolm's first marriage was to  Firmin Desloge 4th, of St. Louis, at the home of Malcolm's parents Mr. and Mrs. George H. Malcolm of Lake Forest, Illinois

At some point in that early 1947 time frame, they were briefly hitched, according to JFK's pal, Charles Spalding, before the Kennedy patriarch took action, according to Hersh. 

He wrote that Spalding told him in his loyalty to Kennedy that the marriage was just a 'high school prank, a bit of daring that went too farâ€¦the kind of joke that Durie would go forâ€¦They went down [to get married] and went through the motions. It was like Halloween. I remember saying to Jack, 'You must be nuts. You're running for president and you're running around getting married.'  

It wasn't until 1957 when an amateur genealogist from New Jersey by the name of Louis L. Blauvelt let the cat out of the bag, so to speak, by printing a private edition of his family's tree. The eleventh generation, entry number 12,427, stated that Durie Malcolm had a third marriage, and the husband was 'John F. Kennedy, son of Joseph P. Kennedy, one time Ambassador to England.'

Jack Kennedy was already commander-in-chief in mid-1961, when the press and the political opposition, including the extreme right, learned of the entry and began hot pursuit. 

As Clark Clifford noted in his memoir, 'Counsel to the President,' Kennedy and his staff 'had tried to deal with the matter by ignoring it; but when the rumor continued to circulate and then surfaced in a few minor periodicals, the President called me.' 

According to the partisan Clifford, Kennedy's response was that years ago he knew Durie 'very briefly,' thought he had just 'two dates' with her, had gone dancing, and attended a football game with her. The bottom line was it was all very innocent, and there never was a marriage.

When JFK's brother, the attorney general Robert Kennedy, learned that powerful Look magazine was investigating, he threatened, 'You print that story and I'll wind up owning Look magazine.' 

He had made the warning to one of the magazine's star reporters, Laura Bergquist, who had much access to the Kennedys, and she had recounted Bobby Kennedy's confrontation in her oral history on file in the JFK Library.

The then-FBI director J. Edgar Hoover had also received intelligence about the reputed marriage, and brought up the subject to the attorney general. Bobby Kennedy responded by saying he hoped reporters would publish the story 'because then we' â€“ meaning the Kennedy clan â€“ 'could all retire for life on what 'we' collect.'

Washington Post editor Ben Bradlee wrote about the Kennedy-Malcolm affair in his own memoir, and recalled that Jackie Kennedy was present when her husband gloatingly confronted him about the marriage story, stating it wasn't true. Bradlee noted that Jackie listened 'with a smile on her face'

When Newsweek's Washington-based reporter Ben Bradlee, later to oversee the Washington Post's Pulitzer Prize-winning Watergate coverage, went after the story of the marriage, he could find no evidence, 

Jack Kennedy told him, 'You haven't got it, Benjy. You're all looking to tag me with some girl, and none of you can do it, because it just isn't there.'

Bradlee, who had been on the outs with his close friend the president, eventually cast serious doubt on the marriage rumor â€“ under pressure from Kennedy - in a piece for Newsweek that was picked up by the Washington Post. As a result, he was back in Kennedy's good graces.

Bradlee wrote about the Kennedy-Malcolm affair in his own memoir, and recalled that Jackie Kennedy was present when her husband gloatingly confronted him about the marriage story, stating it wasn't true.

Bradlee noted that Jackie listened 'with a smile on her face.'

What she really knew behind that smile went to the grave with her.
