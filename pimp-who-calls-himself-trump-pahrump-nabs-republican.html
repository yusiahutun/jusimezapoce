But Hof won handily on Tuesday, defeating Oscarson and getting a concession from the incumbent via text message at 11:21pm. “Congratulations,” Oscarson’s text read. “Thank you,” Hof replied.

Hof’s bid for the seat drew national attention due to his outsize reality television persona, culled from an HBO series called Cathouse that took place in one of several brothels he operates in Nye and Lyon counties.

Laura Billman, Oscarson’s spokeswoman, said her candidate’s campaign felt it had been running behind the entire race. When the unofficial Nye County numbers came in – 2,372 votes for Hof and 1,465 for Oscarson – Billman said they knew the numbers could not be made up in Clark and Lincoln counties.

“Nye County voters don’t like incumbents,” she said.

Nevada was one of five states that held primaries Tuesday – bringing the total to 21 so far heading into the midterm elections in November.

Democrats are hoping to flip enough congressional seats to seize control of the House of Representatives. And in the wake of the #MeToo movement, a record number of women have been running for office.

Hof’s bid comes as a petition drive is underway in Nye and Lyon counties to eliminate their legal brothels.

Activists in the two counties, which have 40 per cent of the state’s brothels, have until Friday to gather enough signatures to qualify for the November ballot.

Some have said the petitions are aimed at Hof. In February, the Nye County sheriff shut down the Hof-owned Love Ranch near Pahrump over building code violations.

At the Bob Ruud Community Center in Pahrump, voter traffic was heavy as people sought the shadiest path possible to cast their ballots. Temperatures hit 106 degrees Fahrenheit (41 degrees C).

Brett Chunley, 45, wore sunglasses and repeatedly wiped his brow outside after casting his vote for Oscarson. He said there was no way he would vote for Hof.

“He was an embarrassment before he ran for office,” Chunley said. “He’s even more of one now.”

He said Hof was ridiculous, noting he’d at one point endorsed Hillary Clinton with his “Hookers for Hillary” campaign. “And now he says he’s a conservative Republican? Please.”

Nevada State Assembly District 36, which primarily covers traditionally Republican Nye County, also includes portions of Lincoln County and a small part of Clark County, for a total population of about 67,000.

Jim Raubolt, a 55-year-old electrician, said he could not bring himself to vote for Hof or Oscarson and cast his ballot for Joseph Bradley, the third Republican candidate, who largely slid under the radar while the other two engaged in their high-profile battle.

But Raubolt said that if Hof won the nomination, he would vote for the pimp in November.

“The Democrats keep electing knuckleheads,” he said.

Sean Hunt, sitting in the back of his white pickup, was distributing white T-shirts that read, “Protect Nye County freedom.” He said he voted for Hof.

“I did it to piss people off,” he said. “It’s the same reason I voted for Trump. I knew it would drive liberals crazy.”

Nye County went for Trump handily in 2016, giving him 68 per cent of the vote – though Nevada overall went for Clinton.

At the Nye County Republican Party headquarters Tuesday night, cardboard cut-outs of Trump and Ronald Reagan were propped against a wall.

A man at the front door was watching conspiracy theorist and far-right agitator Alex Jones on his computer as Republicans filtered in and waited for Hof and other candidates to arrive.

Hof walked in around 7:45pm wearing an American flag T-shirt, blue jeans and red socks, and was presented with a sign painted in red, white and blue that read, “Dennis Hof, the Trump of Pahrump”.

He posed for pictures with it and got a round of applause from the approximately 100 people in the room. Former madam and reality TV personality Heidi Fleiss was there, wearing a T-shirt that read “Vote Hof”.

When it was clear Hof had an insurmountable lead, champagne was brought out. Corks were popped. Pictures were taken. The Nye County Republican Central Committee chairman, Joe Burdzinski, stood before a crowd of about two dozen and looked at Hof.

“You won,” he said.

Hof said he couldn’t have without appearances by Grover Norquist and Roger Stone.

“This is American history,” Hof said. “I feel like right won over might and another RINO (Republican in Name Only) got fired.”
