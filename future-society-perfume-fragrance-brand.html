Future Society Perfume and Fragrance Review

 Posted on April 13, 2024  |  5 minutes  |  863 words  |  Martina Birk

Most perfumes pull from the same pool of floral notes. There’s rose, jasmine, tuberose, gardenia—you get it. Though master perfumers can make magic with just a handful of familiar notes, they are somewhat limited to the boundaries of convention when creating a new fragrance. Today, that all changes, with the launch of a new brand called Future Society, which asks the question: What if, instead, a perfume could smell like extinct flowers?

The idea was born after scientists, using preserved botanical specimens collected at Harvard University’s Herbaria, sequenced the genetic codes of extinct flowers dating back to 1812. Using that data, the brand worked with perfumers like Jérôme Epinette from scent houses Givaudan and Robertet to source a mix of fragrance notes—synthetic, natural, and bioengineered—inspired by the scent molecules of the extinct flowers. The result: six juices that bring the smell of long-gone blooms back to life.

“Our goal wasn’t to solve problems within the fragrance industry or replace ingredients we already use with more sustainable ones,” say Future Society founder and CEO Jasmina Aganovic. “Instead, we set out to make scents we’ve never smelled before and fragrances that were previously not possible to make.”

Just the idea of extinct flower sequencing is thrilling, but the experience of actually smelling each of Future Society’s scents cements the brand as one of the most exciting new launches in recent memory. The fragrances are not only wearable, but also refreshingly unique. It’s as if your brain can’t pick out one particular note with each sniff, because the scents smell like something you’ve never known.

Get to know the six scents and the extinct plants that inspired them:

Floating Forest

SMELLS LIKE: Shorea cuspidata, “a towering tree endemic to Borneo whose branches once grazed the sky at up to 45 meters. Last spotted in the wild in 1998, logging activities in its forest habitat forever took its descendants from the earth.”

Haunted Rose

SMELLS LIKE: Macrostylis villosa subspecies 1 and 2, “a small aromatic shrub with needle-like leaves that once produced clusters of white flowers for long periods of the year throughout South Africa. Its population fragmented in 1960 due to the effects of urbanization on the shrub’s native regions.”

Solar Canopy

SMELLS LIKE: Hibiscadelphus wilderianus, “a Hawaiian endemic species that once covered the Southern Slope of Haleakala but was last observed in 1912. The tree that produced this flower vanished due to deforestation.”

Reclaimed Flame

SMELLS LIKE: Leucadendron grandiflorum, “a species that once flowered on the Western Cape of South Africa. This species has not been spotted since 1960, [the victim of] frequent wildfires spurred on by land development.”

Invisible Woods

SMELLS LIKE: Wendlandia angustifolia, “a small flowering tree that once thrived in India’s Western Ghats mountain range. Due to perpetual drought that overtook the region, its delicate white bloom had not been recorded since 1917.”

Grassland Opera

SMELLS LIKE: Orbexilum stipulatum, “a flowering plant that once flourished on Rock Island in the Falls of the Ohio but was last observed in 1881. The flower is thought to have collapsed when the buffalo ceased migrating across the planes.”

The project of creating these six new scents was also novel for the master perfumers, who had to not only re-create the scent from genetic code but also travel back in time to imagine what the flowers and plants were really like. For example, perfumer Olivia Jan, who works with Givaudan, was transported back to North America’s rural Midwest in the mid-19th century when creating her fresh fragrance Grassland Opera. “The Orbexilum stipulatum flower grew near a waterfall, so I tried to make something wet, green, and lush,” Jan says. Standout favorites among Bazaar editors include Grassland Opera, and also Solar Canopy and Invisible Woods. The Future Society Scent Surrection Set allows you to sample all six for just $35.

“What’s important to us about this brand is not falling into the trope of science in a way that’s overly intellectualized—as if we know everything and can solve anything. We’re not trying to out-science our way out of problems. The the reality is, science cannot bring these flowers back to life in a literal sense,” Aganovic tells Bazaar. “But on the flip side, because of science, we can actually do something that we were never able to do before. And so we should be really grateful and optimistic for that.” With potential new launches including skincare products made from clouds and rainwater, the founder says these six new scents are just the beginning for Future Society.
