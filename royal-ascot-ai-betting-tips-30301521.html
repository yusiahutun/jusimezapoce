Punters to bet a record 300m on Royal Ascot with racing fans banking on AI for winners

 Posted on May 16, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  628 words  |  Aldo Pusey

Bookies reckon punters will bet a record £300million on Royal Ascot - after deploying bots to pick their winners.

Racing fans are turning to websites which use AI to number-crunch stats on horses, jockeys and courses to predict the most likely victors.

The tech has attracted a new wave of gamblers lured in by data suggesting bots are better at betting than humans. In one test an AI punter successfully predicted seven out of 10 winners - a higher hit rate than the best professional gamblers.

Bookmakers said the ability to use Artificial Intelligence to land extra lolly had helped fuel an Ascot betting frenzy. Paddy Power spokesman Paul Binfield said: "We expected in excess of £250 million to be wagered industry wide across the week.

"But there’s a strong possibility that the constant changes and upgrades in technology make that an underestimation. These hugely-exciting new innovations could well mean a record this year."

Simon Clare, of Coral, said bookmakers were themselves now using Ai to protect themselves from the use of betting bots.

"There is a kind of bot versus bot battle going on," he said. "We have to ensure the odds take technological advancements into account. While it is wonderful if technology introduces an a new audience to the joys of racing part of the real fun is studying the form the old-fashioned way and finding your own winner.

"Using a bot to do the hard work for you surely will not give the same degree of satisfaction."

According to gambling website Racing Dudes AI can be used to 'predict statistically accurate outcomes for sporting events’. The first attempt in 1996 was used to work out NFL results with 61% accuracy’.

By 2003 the accuracy prediction rate had gone up to 75% for 205 American Football matches. In 2010 boffins started targeting horse racing with 77% accuracy. That is likely to have increased since.

But the website states: "While gamblers are just attempting to gain an edge with the technology bookies may have already adapted their models with the help of machine learning.

"We can speculate that in the future sports betting will see gamblers betting on horses with no-code AI butting heads against bookies using their own AutoML algorithms in a self-correcting predictive model arms race. Bookmakers also have the advantage of being able to restrict successful gamblers.

"In this case even if you develop your model that performs better than the bookies’, they will eventually exclude you after a string of good predictions."

Bots were not just deployed to find winners at Ascot - they were used to design outfits.

Sci-fi writer Kate Levchuk, 32, used a bot to design her Ladies’ Day dress. The Ukrainian wanted to turn up wearing her nation’s colours. She entered them into an AI program which produced hundreds of potential designs.

Kate, from Odesa, then picked her favourite, had it made by a designer for £2,000 and unveiled the blue, yellow and white creation at Thursday’s meeting.

"It is quite funny to think a robot designed my dress but I think it will become a common thing," she said.

"It was so simple. I was able to enter the colours and theme I wanted and it produced so many possible designs. I then selected the one that appealed, handed it to a dressmaker and now I have a unique outfit that represents my nation. I hope it helps raise awareness about Ukraine ’s plight.

"I feel I wasn’t just wearing a dress to Royal Ascot - I was making a personal statement. It was my small act of defiance."
